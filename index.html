Fundamental Separation

  • Separate: content, presentation, behaviour
  • The backbone is the raw semantics
  • Styling is an indirection to semantics, but a poor one

Target All Humans

  • Modality-independence helps software agents
  • In turn, this helps the full variety of humans using those agents
  • Not just accessibility: internationalisation, Unicode

Principle of Least Power

  • HTML is dumb: that’s good
  • «Powerful languages inhibit information reuse.» (TAG Finding)
  • Use the least powerful language that will adequately capture your semantics

  • HTML trees can be arbitrarily enriched

Fuzzier is Less Noisy

  • Cater to your primary data producers, not consumers
  • Increasing ontological precision beyond beyond the training of data producers (humans) just leads to noise
  • Massive success, very loose: schema.org
  • Decentralised capture (eg. schema:PublicToilet)

Collective Robustness

Postel’s Law

Implementation Drift in a Postel World

Humans are not conservative (at scale).

  1. Authors check content against browser (not tech spec)
  2. Dominant browser has bug (or is just being liberal)
  3. Content relies on bug
  4. Competing browsers have to emulate bug to be usable
  5. Go to 1

Web technology started to change on its own as a collective phenomenon.

Issues

  • People are lazy, computers are buggy
  • Policing and decentralisation do not coexist at scale
  • Content outlives software (by a lot)
  • At any serious scale, you cannot go offline for upgrades

Assume Errors

  • Early hypertext systems tried to prevent errors
  • Best effort scales better, but implies embracing errors
  • Errors should be exceptional (404) and undefined behaviour obliterated

Predictability

  • No behaviour should be undefined in a format
  • Every input maps to an output
  • Paired with testing, this leaves nowhere to drift
  • HTML: pretty much any junk will produce a tree
